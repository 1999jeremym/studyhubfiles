1. What is the Study Hub?

The Study Hub is a website that helps you learn the stuff you need to know for your class. Think of it like a digital study folder that's smarter than a normal folder.

It holds your:

  • Study notes
  • Flashcards (for memorising things)
  • Quiz questions (to test yourself)
  • Slides from lectures
  • Audio recordings
  • Videos

All in one place. For every topic in your class.

The big idea: instead of bouncing between ten different apps, you open one website and everything you need to study is right there.

2. Opening it for the first time

You click the link your classmate sent you. The first time, Claude will ask you to sign in or make a free account — this is because the hub runs on Claude's website. It takes about a minute. Once you're in, you won't have to do it again.

After that, the hub loads and you're ready to go. You don't need to install anything.

The first thing you'll see is the Dashboard. That's the main home page.

Tip: bookmark the hub link in your browser after you log in the first time. Much faster than hunting for the message where someone sent it.

3. The main parts of the hub

The Dashboard (home page)

When you open the hub, you see:

  • The name of the class you're looking at (up top)
  • A daily quote (just for fun)
  • Some stats — how many topics you've studied, your streak, how close your exam is
  • A list of all your modules (chunks of the course, like "Test 1" or "Test 2")
  • Inside each module, a list of topics (like "Cells" or "Microbiology")

Topics

A topic is the smallest unit. It's one subject area you're learning about. For example: "Blood & Haematology" is a topic. Everything about blood — notes, flashcards, questions — lives inside that topic.

Modules

A module is a group of topics that go together. Usually it's what you'll be tested on in one exam. For example, "Test 2" might have topics like Blood, Homeostasis, and Fluid Balance inside it.

4. Switching between classes

At the top of the hub, you'll see the class name. If you're taking more than one class, click that name. A little box pops up showing all your classes — tap any of them to switch.

Important: each class has its own notes, flashcards, and progress. They don't mix. So when you switch from "Health Science 1" to "Foundations of Nursing", you're looking at completely separate content.

5. Opening a topic

Click any topic in the list. You'll see tabs near the top:

  • Learn — the study notes / study guide
  • Slides — any lecture PDFs added for that topic
  • Audio — lecture recordings
  • Podcast — AI-generated summary audio (like NotebookLM)
  • Video — any video links for that topic
  • Flashcards — for memorising key terms
  • Quiz — to test yourself with multiple-choice questions
  • Tutor — an AI chat that helps explain things
  • Assess — AI-powered mastery check (more on this below)
  • Edit — if you want to add your own stuff

You don't have to use all of them. Click whichever fits what you want to do right now.

6. The study modes explained

Learn mode

Just reading notes. Open it, read through. The notes usually have headings, bullet points, and sometimes images.

Flashcards mode

A card shows you a question or term on the front. You try to remember the answer, then click or tap to flip the card and see if you got it right. After flipping, you tell the hub if you got it right or wrong. This helps it figure out which cards you need to see again soon.

Why this works: your brain learns better when it has to dig for the answer instead of just reading it. It feels harder, but it sticks.

Quiz mode

Multiple-choice questions. You pick an answer, you see if it was right or wrong, and there's usually an explanation. At the end, you get a score.

Tutor mode

This is a chat window. You type a question ("can you explain how kidneys filter blood?") and the AI tutor explains it to you in plain language. You can keep asking follow-up questions like you would with a real tutor.

Assess mode

This is the most interesting one. Instead of a normal quiz, the AI asks you a question, you type your answer in your own words, and it tells you what level you're at (L1 to L5 — see the next section). It also tells you what you got right, what you missed, and what to study next. It's like having a teacher check your work.

7. Mastery levels (L1 to L5)

Every topic has a mastery level. This tells you how well you know it. There are five levels — you climb them in order.

  • L1 — Recall: can you name or define the thing? (Easiest)
  • L2 — Understand: can you explain the process step-by-step?
  • L3 — Analyse: can you explain how it's controlled or regulated?
  • L4 — Apply: can you work out what happens when something goes wrong clinically?
  • L5 — Integrate: can you link it with other body systems and explain a full scenario? (Hardest)

You don't have to reach L5 on everything. For exams, L3 or L4 on most topics is usually solid.

Tip: use Assess mode on a topic you think you know well. It's a good reality-check.

8. Adding your own content

Most of the content in the hub was added by whoever set it up. But you can add your own stuff to any topic — your notes stay private to you, they don't overwrite what anyone else sees.

To add your own:

  1. Open a topic
  2. Click the Edit tab
  3. Paste your own notes in the study guide box
  4. Add flashcards by clicking "+ Add" under Flashcards
  5. Add quiz questions the same way
  6. Paste a link to a video, audio file, or PDF if you want

Everything saves automatically. You don't have to click a save button every time.

Heads up: audio, video, and PDF files can't play directly inside the hub if they're linked from Google Drive. Use a GitHub Pages link if you want them to work properly — ask the person who set up your hub if you're not sure how.

9. Common questions

Will I lose my progress if I close the browser?

No. Everything saves automatically to your browser's storage. Open the hub again tomorrow and it'll all be there.

Can other people see my notes?

No. The notes you add in Edit mode are private to you. Other people have their own version of the hub with their own notes.

What's the difference between "Published" content and "My" content?

Published content is the shared stuff — flashcards and questions that everyone using the hub sees. It shows a small 🔒 icon in Edit mode. You can't change it directly because it's for everyone. Your own added content is only for you and you can edit or delete it anytime.

I deleted something and it came back. Why?

You probably tried to delete something that's part of the shared published content. Those have the 🔒 lock symbol — the way to remove them is to ask the hub's owner to update the published content for everyone.

What does the streak count mean?

It shows how many days in a row you've used the hub to study something. Keeping a streak going is a good nudge to study a little every day instead of cramming.

Should I aim for L5 on every topic?

No. That's actually a bad goal — you'd burn out. Aim for solid L3 or L4 on most things, and L5 only on the topics the exam leans hardest on.

Is this better than just using my notebook?

For some things, yes: flashcards force you to recall stuff from memory, which makes it stick better than re-reading notes. The AI tutor can explain things in different ways until it clicks. For other things, a notebook is still fine. Use whichever is helping you actually learn.

The Study Hub works best when you use it little and often. Five minutes of flashcards every day beats two hours the night before the exam.
